# Code of Conduct

Iris Code is a small open-source project. We want everyone who shows up to feel welcome and respected. The rules are short and the spirit matters more than the letter.

## What we expect

- **Be kind.** Assume the person on the other end is trying their best.
- **Be honest.** Say what you actually think; let others disagree.
- **Be helpful.** New contributors get pointers, not lectures.
- **Stay on topic.** Issues and PRs are about Iris Code, not the broader internet.

## What we won't tolerate

- Harassment, personal attacks, or sustained derailing.
- Doxing or sharing private information without consent.
- Sexualized comments or imagery in any project channel.
- Pile-ons, brigading, or coordinated dunking.
- Repeated bad-faith engagement after a warning.

These rules apply in every project space: issues, PRs, Discussions, and any other forum that bears the project name.

## Reporting

If you see something off, open a **private security advisory** on the GitHub repository at
<https://github.com/oorbitproductivity-oss/iriscode/security/advisories/new> and tag it as a code-of-conduct report. Reports go directly to the maintainers. Include links, dates, and what happened. We will respond within 72 hours.

If a maintainer is the subject of the report, address it to another maintainer.

## What happens after a report

The maintainers will:

1. Acknowledge the report within 72 hours.
2. Investigate quietly. We may ask follow-up questions.
3. Decide on a response — anything from a private word, to a public correction, to removing someone from project spaces.
4. Communicate the decision back to the reporter.

Reporters' identities are kept private. We will not punish people for reporting in good faith, even if the report turns out to be unfounded.

## Scope

This code applies to every contributor: maintainers, sponsors, casual visitors, and people just lurking. The same rules apply to everyone.

## Inspirations

This document borrows ideas from the [Contributor Covenant](https://www.contributor-covenant.org/) and from the codes of conduct of many open-source projects that have come before. It is intentionally short — the goal is something people will actually read.
